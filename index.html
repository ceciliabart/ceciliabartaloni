Cecilia Bartaloni

I contribute to the design and development of undergraduate and postgraduate programmes at IPU New Zealand. My academic background is in Tourism Marketing, and my research explores how emotions, memory, and technology shape memorable tourism experiences. Grounded in consumer behaviour and experience economy theories, my work aims to inform marketing strategy and support digital innovation in tourism. I completed a PhD in Management and Economics at the Free University of Bozen-Bolzano in 2024.

Research Papers

Are tech-savvy tourists eco-friendly? Impact of intention to use sustainable technologies on willingness to pay for smart hotels

Published on the Italian Journal of Marketing with Valerio Veglio, 2025

Smart hotel technologies, such as smart environments and location-based mobile notifications could be used to encourage sustainable behaviours. However, hotel guests might not enjoy interacting with these technologies, while hotel managers might see them as a cost. The aim of the paper is to explore the interrelationship between the hedonic value of smart hotels, and customer intention to use sustainable technologies. The results show that customers displaying a higher affinity for technology are more likely to associate positive anticipated emotions with smart hotels and to adopt sustainable technologies, but not to pay for them.

Unpacking the affective dimension of memorable hospitality experiences

Published on the International Journal of Hospitality Management with Linda Osti and Charles Frederick Hofacker, 2025

Memorable experiences have received considerable attention in tourism research. Yet, there is a lack of a consensus among scholars regarding the elements within the servicescape that contribute to their creation. Based on this research gap, the present study aims at understanding which elements within the servicescape have a greater impact on guests’ emotions when recalling memorable hotel experiences.The results show that the recollection of negative emotions in memorable experiences appears to be affected by the physical and social dimension of the service environment and influenced by the fading affect bias.

Are customers ready to be smart? Designing smart hotel experiences

Published on Tourism and Hospitality Management with Marco Alderighi, 2024

This paper aims to understand how smart hotels can be conceptualised based on customer preferences. To achieve this goal, the paper conceptualises smart hotels as a service network in which the customer comes into contact with a variety of onsite technological and traditional touchpoints. This conceptualisation allows the researcher to observe customer preferences at different touchpoints and identify the technologies that are central to the development of the customer experience.The results show that the ideal customer journey includes both traditional and technological touchpoints and that artificial intelligence is of central importance for the development of the smart hotel concept.
